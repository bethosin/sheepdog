Watch Interview

Championship Excellence

Julie Hill represents the traditional path to sheepdog excellence, a legacy shaped by decades of commitment and achievement. Her résumé includes titles such as International Supreme Champion (1996), Reserve International Supreme Champion (2006), International Brace Champion, Scottish Brace Champion, and Scottish National Champion. Coming from a shepherding background, Hill embodies the sport’s deep agricultural heritage. Listen to our interview with Julie Hill discussing her journey from traditional shepherding to championship success. Yet Hill also bridges generations: in 2021, she co-founded Sheepdog School, the world’s first sheepdog training app featuring more than 150 instructional videos, proving how seasoned experts are expanding access for aspiring handlers who lack traditional farming connections. Her training style emphasizes partnership over dominance, fostering dogs that think independently and solve problems—an approach that resonates strongly with younger handlers seeking deeper, more meaningful relationships with working animals.

Knowledge Transfer Through Technology

In 2021, Emma Gray and Ewan co-founded Sheepdog School to bridge the gap between elite, championship-level expertise and aspiring handlers around the world. The platform includes more than 150 in-depth training videos covering complete journeys from puppy to trial winner. Lessons span starting young dogs on sheep, building foundations, progressing through skill levels, solving common behavioral challenges, and preparing for competitive trials.

Website Link: https://sheepdog-school.com — the world’s first comprehensive sheepdog training app.

Sheepdog School has earned industry recognition for its innovative approach. Beyond convenience, it represents the democratization of knowledge: young people without farm backgrounds or access to local mentors can now learn directly from proven champions. The structured lessons allow learners to study at their own pace, replay complex techniques, and build understanding before applying skills in real work.

Emma Gray and Ewan’s training philosophy emphasizes partnership over dominance. Rooted in pressure-and-release principles and canine psychology, their methods teach handlers to communicate in ways dogs naturally understand. This encourages dogs to control their energy, take initiative, and work confidently even when they cannot see the sheep—producing not just obedient workers, but thinking partners capable of independent decision-making.

Chelsea Elkin — International Supreme Young Handler (Age 14)

Rising Star

A Rising Champion

Chelsea Elkin - Elite Youth Achievement Fourteen-year-old Chelsea Elkin won the 2025 International Supreme Young Handler Championship, proving the new generation competes at the highest levels. Working with her dog Marchup Jan at the prestigious Brynkinalt Estate, Elkin displayed skill and composure that defies her age.

Her calm handling, sharp timing, and strong partnership with her dog earned admiration from worldwide audiences.

Why Her Story Matters

Young competitors like Chelsea prove the sport welcomes and celebrates new generations. Her victory inspires young handlers everywhere.

Farmer's Guardian Feature

Youth at Elite Levels

The 2025 International Supreme Young Handler championship crowned 14-year-old Chelsea Elkin, demonstrating that youth and elite performance aren't mutually exclusive. Working with her dog Marchup Jan at the prestigious Brynkinalt Estate in Chirk, Wrexham, Elkin displayed the skill, composure, and partnership that defines championship-level trialing. As the announcement noted, such achievement "at such a young age is truly inspiring the future is very bright for Chelsea and Marchup Jan."³

At just 14, Elkin's success proves the sport actively welcomes and celebrates young talent. The International Sheepdog Society's dedicated young handler categories provide pathways for emerging competitors to test themselves at the highest levels, competing alongside and often outperforming handlers with decades more experience. Young handlers aren't tokens in this sport they're winning internationally and raising standards.

Youth & Accessibility

If you scroll through Instagram's #sheepdogtrials or search Facebook for sheepdog groups, you'll find something unexpected: young faces. Teenagers filming training, twenty-somethings analyzing runs, university students balancing coursework with weekend competitions. The stereotype of aging rural sport doesn't match reality.

Victoria Graham — Dedication & Growth in the Sport

Inspiration

From Childminder to Open Trial Winner

Victoria Graham entered the sport after watching her friend Tom Rome work his dogs. Inspired, she began training under mentors like Jeremy Martin.

Her first dog Pip guided her through early learning stages from novice events to open competition victories.

Reaching the English National

By 2019, Graham achieved her dream of competing at the English National, calling the experience "unforgettable and deeply special."

Building Her Own Path

She later trained her own dog Teal, renting a local field and raising Hebridean sheep specifically for training purposes.

Official Announcement

Graham bred and trained her own dog, Teal, renting a small field nearby to start a flock of Hebridean sheep specifically for training. Teal's successful first nursery season placing most weekends, winning a nursery championship, and making the reserve list for the English Nursery Final proved that dedicated training outside traditional farming contexts produces competitive results.

Graham's story demonstrates accessible entry points—you don't need a farm, just access to sheep through rental or arrangement, mentorship from experienced handlers willing to share knowledge, and commitment to the partnership. Her progression from complete beginner to English National competitor spans nine years of consistent effort, showing that expertise develops through dedication rather than requiring agricultural background.

Accessible Pathways

The next generation of handlers often comes from unexpected backgrounds, proving the sport welcomes anyone with dedication. Victoria Graham, a childminder from Cumbria, exemplifies how sheepdog trialing fits diverse lifestyles. After watching her friend Tom Rome work his dogs, Graham was captivated: "I thought his skill was amazing and I knew I wanted to try it for myself."⁴

Over nine years, Graham progressed from nervous beginner to open trial winner. Her first dog, Pip bred by Rome and trained by Jeremy Martin "put up with all my mistakes" as Graham learned through new handler and novice trials. By 2019, they won their first open competition. Graham achieved her dream of competing at the English National: "I will never forget walking out onto that trial field with her, such a special experience and one that I will always treasure."
