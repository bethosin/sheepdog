Get Started Handlers

Introduction

The whistle's sharp call has echoed across British hillsides since 1873, when shepherd R.J. Lloyd Price organized the first recorded sheepdog trial in Bala, Wales. That gathering of ten handlers testing their dogs' working ability sparked a tradition now spanning continents and generations. Today's sheepdog trials competitive events where handlers use whistles and commands to guide Border Collies in precisely moving sheep through obstacles demand the same skills shepherds perfected centuries ago. Yet scroll through Instagram's #sheepdogtrials, and you'll find something unexpected: young faces documenting training sessions, analyzing competition runs, proving this heritage sport thrives in modern hands. Fourteen-year-old champions. Childminders turned competitors. Digital training platforms created by world record holders. The whistle connects them all past, present, and future speaking the same language.

Patient Excellence

Traditional handlers understand excellence cannot be rushed. Young dogs need 18-24 months of foundation work before serious training begins. This patient philosophy extends to reading livestock— experienced handlers develop an intuitive sense for when sheep are settled versus anxious, when pressure helps versus scatters, and how terrain and weather affect behavior. This “feel,” built through thousands of hours, separates competent handlers from champions.

Modern training aids enhance learning but cannot replace deep understanding of animal behavior, timing, and environmental factors that experienced handlers embody. Digital platforms like Sheepdog School bridge this gap, allowing expertise to be studied, reviewed, and internalized at an individual pace while maintaining connection to proven methods developed through decades of competitive success.

Growing a Heritage Sport

What Makes Traditional Sports Thrive?

Sports development experts understand what it takes for heritage activities to attract new generations while still preserving their authentic character. Research across sports such as rock climbing, skateboarding, and equestrian disciplines shows clear, consistent patterns for successful youth engagement.

Three key factors are essential for growth:

  • Accessible entry points that reduce initial barriers for beginners.
  • Visible youth achievement at elite levels to inspire upcoming participants.
  • Celebrated youth success that signals genuine community support and belonging.

Sheepdog trials demonstrate all three principles effectively.

  • Accessible Entry Points: Victoria Graham's story exemplifies this principle. Without farming background, she rented training space and progressed from complete beginner to English National competitor in nine years. Online platforms like Sheepdog School democratize championship-level knowledge, removing the traditional requirement for local mentorship or agricultural connections.
  • Visible Youth Achievement: Chelsea Elkin winning the International Supreme Young Handler Championship at 14 sends a powerful message: youth isn't a barrier, it's an asset. When teenagers compete successfully at the highest levels, peers recognize the sport values their participation.
  • Celebrated Youth Success: : Chelsea Elkin’s International Supreme Young Handler victory at age 14 isn’t just an individual achievement, it’s strategic visibility. When mainstream sheepdog media celebrates teenage champions with the same enthusiasm as veteran winners, it signals to young observers that the sport genuinely values their participation. Dedicated young handler categories at major championships mirror successful youth competition structures from other sports, providing appropriate competition levels while creating clear pathways to open classes.
  • Community Welcome: The International Sheepdog Society's dedicated young handler categories aren't token gestures they're serious competitions crowning world champions. Social media communities using #sheepdogtrials connect young handlers globally, creating support networks that transcend geographic isolation. What's changed isn't the sport's core values the partnership between handler and dog remains unchanged. What's evolved is accessibility. Knowledge once requiring apprenticeship is now available online. Competition opportunities once requiring farm ownership now welcome rented-field trainers. Communities once existing only at physical trials now connect globally through digital platforms.

Your Path Forward

For young people intrigued by sheepdog trials, the pathway is accessible. Here’s how to start:

This Month — Observe and Learn

Attend local trials—watching builds understanding faster than videos alone. Notice how handlers position themselves, when they intervene versus letting dogs work independently, how sheep respond to different pressures and distances, how weather and terrain affect strategies. The International Sheepdog Society provides comprehensive trial calendars and regional contacts at isds.org.uk, making it easy to find events near you.⁷

This Year — Begin Your Journey

If you have access to a suitable dog (Border Collies or other herding breeds), seek mentorship from experienced handlers. The ISDS maintains connections to instructors nationwide who welcome novices with appropriate dogs and genuine commitment. Starting with professional guidance prevents developing bad habits difficult to correct later.

If you don't own sheep, explore options:

  • Rent small fields like Victoria Graham (even urban areas have hobby farmers willing to arrange access)
  • Join local shepherding associations that maintain training facilities
  • Volunteer at farms in exchange for training access
  • Attend training center courses regularly until establishing your own arrangements

Consider volunteering at trials to understand competition structure and meet participants. Helpers are always needed for gate-keeping, pen-building, timekeeping, and event organization roles providing insider perspective while contributing to the community. Many successful handlers started as volunteers, observing closely before competing themselves.

Document your journey if comfortable with social media. Many handlers built supportive online communities by sharing honest progress mistakes included. This transparency helps others while connecting you with people facing similar challenges. You don't need polished professional content; authenticity resonates more than perfection.

Long-Term — Championship Aspirations

→ The 2026 World Sheepdog Trials (September 16-20, Lauder, Scotland) showcases what's possible when handlers master the craft. This prestigious event, held every three years, brings together competitors from 29+ countries for five days of intense competition. The championship includes dedicated young handler categories providing international pathways proof that youth divisions aren't minor leagues but serious competitions crowning world champions. ⁸

Past youth champions started exactly where you are now curious, committed, ready to learn. Chelsea Elkin at 14, winning major championships. Victoria Graham, progressing from complete beginner to English National competitor in nine years. Julie Hill, building from early interest into multiple championship titles and world records. Their journeys differ in detail but share common elements: patience through early frustrations, commitment through setbacks, celebration of small progresses, and deep investment in the partnership.

Watch previous championships on YouTube the ISDS and various handlers maintain video archives. Study top handlers' techniques: how they position for different course challenges, when they intervene versus trusting dogs' judgment, how they maintain composure under pressure, how they adapt strategies when initial plans fail. Competition footage provides education no book can offer, revealing the relationship between pressure, distance, and livestock response through repeated viewing.

The pathway from beginner to championship competitor isn't easy it requires years of patient work, countless training hours, inevitable frustrations, and sustained commitment. But it's achievable. The handlers competing at elite levels aren't superhuman. They're people who decided to answer the whistle's call and maintained dedication through challenges.

The whistle has sounded for 150 years, connecting generations across continents. Your answer begins with single step: attending that first trial, contacting that local handler, watching that training video with genuine curiosity. The partnership awaits
